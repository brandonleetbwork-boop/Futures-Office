SMEs are not Mini-MEs

A State vs Stage Perspective of Enterprise Growth

Brandon Lee
7 min read
Enterprise & Growth

We have a tendency, in enterprise policy, to picture the SME as a compressed multinational. Smaller balance sheet, smaller headcount, smaller reach — but the same underlying structure, just running at a lower resolution. Help it grow and it will eventually resemble the thing we are picturing. This image is compelling. It is also, I want to argue, the source of a quiet but consequential error in how we identify which firms to support and to what ends.

The Economic Strategy Review recommendations, released earlier this week, set out a goal for Singapore's enterprise ecosystem that is both clear and right: more of our homegrown companies should start, scale, and succeed globally. Thrust 4 of the recommendations names this directly — a more dynamic enterprise ecosystem, more Singapore-based companies with the reach and capability to compete on the world stage. This is the right ambition. What I want to examine is the instrument: the implicit framework we use to select which firms are candidates for that journey, and whether it is actually fit for purpose.

Currently, the dominant selection logic works primarily from stage and size. How old is the firm? How fast is it growing? What sector is it in? What is its revenue trajectory? Enterprise support programmes already apply differentiated logic within this frame — different instruments for different profiles, different schemes for different sectors. The question is not whether we differentiate. It is whether we are differentiating on the right variable. These are measurements of output — they tell us what the firm has produced so far. They tell us much less about what the firm structurally is: the nature of its internal coherence, the kind of substrate on which it is built, and therefore what it is genuinely capable of becoming, and through what path. Stage and size are the easy measures. State is the harder one — and I would argue the one that matters more when we are trying to decide not just whether to support a firm, but how, and toward what ends.

This is not an abstract distinction. Let me try to make it concrete.

The Spaghetti Tower

There is real design intelligence here — the founder understands how the pieces fit, the structure has its own logic, the firm is genuinely responsive. It can absorb energy and reconfigure quickly. The problem is the substrate. Spaghetti is flexible but not load-bearing, and resource injection into a Spaghetti Tower does not produce a stronger structure. It produces a bigger spaghetti tower — and a bigger one is more fragile at greater scale.

The Container House

Other firms look quite different. The Container House has solved the coherence problem. It is well-structured, reliably operational, genuinely resilient. It can legitimately grow through replication at regional or even global scale — the container can travel. But asking it to transform into something structurally different is another matter entirely, and that is where significant surgery becomes relevant if the ambition is for the container to become something it was not designed to be.

The Lego Set

The Lego Set is the structural state that maps most cleanly onto the ESR's language of global scale. The firm has reached a level of modular coherence where it can build larger — responsively, in different configurations, across different markets — in proportion to the energy and resources committed to it. The pieces fit; the design is extensible without needing to be reinvented. This is the firm our current enterprise development logic tends to imagine when it talks about homegrown companies with global potential. The problem is that not many SMEs are in this structural state. And treating the others as if they were produces disappointment all round.

The Beaver Dam

Then there is the Beaver Dam. This is the firm that has evolved, over time, into something complex, coherent, and deeply fitted to its environment. It absorbs a great deal — capital, relationships, institutional knowledge — and converts it productively. But what it has built is not easily reproduced elsewhere. The complexity is the coherence: unpick it and you risk destroying the thing that makes it work. I think we systematically undervalue firms like this. They often do jobs that no other firm is doing and that the domestic ecosystem and population depends on. Treating the Beaver Dam as a failed Lego Set is not just analytically wrong. It tends to produce interventions that either have no effect or actively destabilise a firm that was, by any honest measure, a success.

Phase

Materials science offers a useful frame here: phase. The same iron, under different conditions, exists in structurally distinct phases — ferrite, austenite, martensite — each with different properties, different stability conditions, and non-trivial transition paths between them. Phase is not a quality judgment. It is a description of current structural condition and what it implies about what the material can do, and how it can change. Not every phase can transition to every other without significant energy input. Some transitions, attempted without the right conditions, simply fail. Others produce something that looks like the target state but behaves differently under load.

SME structural states could work similarly. A Spaghetti Tower can become a Lego Set, but the path runs through structural refactoring — and it is not a short path, nor one that responds to conventional grant logic. Container Houses can be replicated and planted where their design fits the surroundings. A Beaver Dam probably should not be pushed toward replication at all; the right intervention is support for what it already is. Recognising that these are structurally different states, not just different points on the same growth curve, changes what good support design actually looks like.

The policy implication here is not a new classification system. The implication is that the questions we ask at the point of support identification need to change. Less: how fast is this firm growing, and how do we accelerate it? More: what kind of firm is this, what does its structure make possible, and what does a well-matched intervention actually look like here? That is a more demanding question. It requires assessors and programme designers who can read a firm's structural condition, not just its recent financials. It requires support products designed for the intervention logic appropriate to each state. And it requires a willingness to say clearly that some firms are not candidates for global scale, not because they have fallen short, but because their structural state points toward a different contribution and a different form of success.

Singapore's enterprise ecosystem does not need to be a monoculture of aspiring multinationals. It needs Lego Sets with genuine global ambition, and the conditions to support that ambition seriously. But it also needs Beaver Dams: firms that hold essential domestic functions, that carry institutional knowledge that cannot easily be imported, and that represent a different form of enterprise excellence. An ecosystem without enough of the former will struggle to compete globally. An ecosystem without enough of the latter will be more fragile than it looks.

More Singapore companies, succeeding at scale, on the world stage is an ambition that we can get behind. Getting there means being more discerning about state. Which firms can make that journey? From where? Through what path? And which firms are doing something equally important, right here, that we should stop trying to convert and start trying to sustain?

That is the question a stage-based lens cannot answer. A state-based one might.

Brandon Lee will be the future Chief Futures at the soon to be established Skills and Workforce Development Agency (SWDA). The views expressed here are his own.
